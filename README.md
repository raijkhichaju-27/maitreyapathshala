# Parent education
Parents Education and Teacher Training
1.	Parents Education
Raising children who will become happy, healthy, thriving adults is one of the most challenging. In Waldorf Education, our school we designed to engage not only the students but their families as well. We believe in meaningful relationships, where our trained teachers work with families over multiple years of understanding how to best support each child. 
Maitreya Pathshala also offers a regular parent education series, where we discuss topics such as 
•	Understanding the Child development 
•	Grade Specific Parenting Sessions
•	How teaching the hand, heart, and hands builds tolerance, resiliency and flexibility in students
•	Why Waldorf waits on academics
•	Simplicity Parenting approach in your home
•	Waldorf’s approach to media and technology
 Events like Spring Fair, house building, Class 8/9 play, festival celebrations are perfect example of the Waldorf Community coming together to celebrate, contribute, and model for our children the importance of being a part of a connected community. Every person – teacher, parent, grandparent, student plays a role to make every event a success. 
We know that working in close relationship to parents is a powerful and important way to connect school life and home life. From home visits, one to one parent sessions, to parents – teachers conference, these are all opportunities to support the whole family and further the education of our students.

2.	Teachers Training
Teacher education at Maitreya Pathshala is not a one-time induction – it is continuous evolving process that supports every teacher in becoming an inspiring mentor. We believe that the quality of education depends on the teachers who teach or deliver it. So our educators are not only facilitators of learning but constantly evolving to meet the needs of the children they serve.
Our faculty members are trained with 
•	Principles of Waldorf education
•	Age specific pedagogy and holistic pedagogy
•	Artistic exploration, emotional awareness, social sensitivity and physical development through practical and outdoor experience
•	Teachers aware of daily rhythm of school to cultivate creativity, critical thinking, empathy and resilience in their students
Maitreya Pathshala committed to continuous growth of the teachers

Journey
Foundation and Inception
In 2011 the seed of anthroposophy sprout in Pokhara with the biodynamic farm with 8 acres of land with the great heart of Babita Tulachan Kayastha. She took in her will the thought of anthroposophical work in Pokhara. She was supported by Ritman Gurung and Bishnu Kumari Sherchan to begin the biodynamic farm. The biodynamic farm began to supply its products to the community. The farm created a small anthroposophical community with program, seminars, workshop and study. 
The anthroposophical community organically requested for the Waldorf Kindergarten. So, the door of Waldorf education opened in 2014 with 15 children and 3 teachers in Pokhara. Since then, every year one grade upgraded and now we have kindergarten to grade 10.
In 2015 we started our grade 1 with Ritman Gurung as a class teacher. In 2022 he finished his first cycle of class teaching.
In 2023 we have upgrated our school with high school with class 9 and 10.
In 2025 three students of our school attended the SEE board examination of Nepal. Three of them passed the examination.
Waldorf Kindergarten
Waldorf Kindergarten at Maitreya Pathshala
A Place Where Childhood Blooms
At Maitreya Pathshala — the Pokhara Waldorf School — our kindergarten is a sanctuary of wonder, warmth, and unhurried childhood. Nestled in the foothills of the Himalayas, we offer children ages 3 to 6 an education rooted in the rhythms of nature, the magic of play, and the wisdom of the Waldorf tradition.
________________________________________
The Heart of Waldorf Early Childhood Education
Waldorf kindergarten is guided by a simple, profound belief: childhood is not a preparation for life — it is life. Children learn not through formal instruction or screens, but through imaginative play, seasonal rhythms, artistic activities, and loving imitation of the adults around them.
In our kindergarten, the child's whole being — body, heart, and spirit — is nourished equally. We do not rush children into reading or writing. Instead, we give them something far more precious: time to be fully, joyfully young.
________________________________________
A Day Filled with Rhythm and Beauty
Each day at Maitreya Pathshala follows a gentle, predictable rhythm — like breathing in and breathing out. Children feel safe and grounded when they know what comes next.
A typical day flows through free play, circle time with seasonal songs and verses, artistic activities such as watercolor painting, beeswax modeling, and crafts, cooking and gardening, storytelling, outdoor play, and shared meals. This rhythm reflects the great patterns of nature and provides children with an inner security that supports healthy development.
________________________________________
Learning Through Play
In Waldorf education, play is the child's work. Free, imaginative, unstructured play is the most important activity of early childhood — it builds creativity, problem-solving, social skills, emotional resilience, and the foundation for all future learning.
Our kindergarten is filled with simple, natural materials — wooden toys, silk scarves, pine cones, stones, beeswax, and wool — that invite the imagination to breathe and create, unlike plastic toys that leave nothing to the child's own creative spirit.
________________________________________
Rooted in Nature and the Seasons
At Maitreya Pathshala, we celebrate the turning of the seasons with festivals, stories, and crafts that connect children to the living world around them. The gardens, the mountain air, the changing light — all become teachers. Children learn to observe, to care for growing things, and to feel themselves as part of something larger.
________________________________________
The Role of the Teacher
The Waldorf kindergarten teacher is not a lecturer but a loving, present guide — someone who models work, creativity, and inner calm, inspiring children through doing rather than directing. Artistic work, storytelling, and handwork are woven into the teacher's own daily life in the classroom, so children can naturally absorb and imitate what they see.
________________________________________
No Academics — And Here's Why
Waldorf kindergarten is intentionally free of academic instruction. Research in child development, as well as Waldorf founder Rudolf Steiner's insights, show that young children learn primarily through their senses and movement, not abstract thinking. Premature intellectual instruction can actually interfere with healthy development. Children who are not pushed to read and write early often become stronger, more joyful learners in the long run.
At Maitreya Pathshala, we trust the child's natural unfolding.

Waldorf Classes 1–8 at Maitreya Pathshala
The Journey Through Childhood — A Living Education
At Maitreya Pathshala — the Pokhara Waldorf School — our Classes 1 through 8 offer children a deeply meaningful, artistically rich, and developmentally attuned education. Over eight years, the same class teacher walks alongside the children, guiding them through a curriculum that grows and deepens as they do — meeting each child at exactly the right moment in their unfolding.
This is not education as information transfer. It is education as transformation.
________________________________________
One Teacher, Eight Years — The Class Teacher Journey
One of the most distinctive features of Waldorf education is the class teacher — a single dedicated teacher who stays with the same group of children from Class 1 all the way through Class 8. This creates a profound bond of trust, deep knowledge of each child, and a continuous, coherent educational journey. The teacher becomes a second family elder — someone who truly knows and believes in every child.
________________________________________
The Rhythm of Learning: Main Lesson Blocks
Rather than switching subjects every 45 minutes, Waldorf students engage in main lesson blocks — three to four week deep dives into a single subject each morning. This allows children to truly immerse themselves, sleep on what they've learned, and return to it with fresh eyes. Knowledge settles deeply rather than being crammed and forgotten.
Every main lesson integrates storytelling, artistic work, movement, discussion, and written work — engaging the whole child, not just the intellect.
________________________________________
The Curriculum, Class by Class
Class 1 — Age 6–7: The World as Fairy Tale
The threshold between kindergarten and school life is crossed with warmth and story.
Children enter Class 1 when they are truly ready — physically, emotionally, and developmentally. Learning begins through fairy tales, which speak directly to the child's imagination and inner life. Letters and numbers are not introduced as abstract symbols but as living images — ";" emerges from a serpent, "n" from the wave of waters. Arithmetic is felt through movement, rhythm, and story. Form drawing — the flowing of lines and curves — develops the hand and eye for writing and geometry to come.
________________________________________
Class 2 — Age 7–8: Fables, Legends, and the World of Contrasts
The child begins to sense that the world holds both darkness and light.
Stories now become fables from around the world — Aesop, Panchatantra, Jataka tales — which speak to the child's growing moral awareness. Saints' and heroes' legends balance these with ideals of goodness and courage. Numeracy and literacy deepen. The contrast between the noble and the comic, the saint and the trickster, reflects the child's own inner experience of opposites.
________________________________________
Class 3 — Age 8–9: The Human Being in the World
A pivotal year — children begin to feel separate from the world for the first time.
Around age nine, children experience what Waldorf calls the "nine-year change" — a new sense of self and separation from the paradise of early childhood. The curriculum meets this with stories of the ancient time stories (like Bounism, and Old Testament) and creation myths, grounding children in the great human questions: Where do we come from? How do we build shelter, grow food, make clothing? Practical subjects like farming, building, and measurement are introduced — connecting children back to the earth and human community.
________________________________________
Class 4 — Age 9–10: Local Worlds — Nature and Norse Mythology
The child's horizon expands outward from self to community and cosmos.
Norse mythology/Stories of heaven (such as Indra, Barun) — with its vivid, dramatic, and deeply human gods — speaks powerfully to the Class 4 child's emerging individuality and sense of struggle. Local geography and natural history bring the child into relationship with the land they live on. Fractions in mathematics mirror the theme of division and wholeness. Zoology is studied in connection to the human being.
________________________________________
Class 5 — Age 10–11: Ancient Civilizations and the Age of Grace
A year of beauty and balance — often called the most harmonious year of childhood.
Class 5 is considered a golden year. Children study the great ancient civilizations — India, Persia, Mesopotamia, Egypt, and Greece — in vivid narrative form, living into the worldviews of each culture. Greek mythology and the ideals of ancient Athens speak to the child's own sense of harmony and beauty. Botany connects children to the plant world. Geometry becomes freehand, precise, and elegant. Athletics echo the ancient Olympic spirit.
________________________________________
Class 6 — Age 11–12: Rome, Physics, and the Awakening of Cause and Effect
Thinking sharpens. The child begins to meet the world as it truly is.
The Roman world — its law, empire, and practicality — mirrors the Class 6 student's own increasingly logical and critical mind. Physics is introduced for the first time through direct experience: acoustics, optics, heat, and magnetism are explored phenomenologically — students observe first, then form concepts. Business mathematics, mineralogy, and world geography deepen the sense of an ordered, interconnected world.
________________________________________
Class 7 — Age 12–13: The Renaissance, Exploration, and the Age of Discovery
The individual awakens — questioning, adventuring, pushing boundaries.
Adolescence begins to stir, and the curriculum meets it with stories of bold individuals who changed the world — Renaissance artists, scientists, and explorers. Students study the voyages of discovery, the Reformation, and the birth of modern science. Chemistry is introduced. Algebra and geometry become more rigorous. Creative writing flourishes as students develop their own unique voice.
________________________________________
Class 8 — Age 13–14: The Modern World — Revolution, Industry, and the Human Being
The young person stands at the threshold of the wider world.
Class 8 brings the curriculum fully into the modern era — revolutions, industrialization, and the complex forces that have shaped the world the students are about to enter. History, economics, science, and technology are studied together as an interconnected whole. The Class 8 Play — written, designed, and performed entirely by the students — is a major milestone, a creative culmination of eight years together. At graduation, students and teacher part, ready for the next chapter.
Class also prepare for BLE examination.
________________________________________
Across All Eight Years
Artistic Life — Painting, drawing, sculpture, music, drama, and handwork are not extras — they are woven into every subject, every day. Artistic engagement develops thinking, feeling, and willpower in equal measure.
Eurythmy — A unique movement art form developed within Waldorf education, eurythmy brings language and music into visible gesture. It develops coordination, listening, social awareness, and a deep feeling for rhythm and form.
Handwork and Practical Skills — From knitting in Class 1 to woodwork and bookbinding in later classes, practical handwork develops fine motor skills, patience, creativity, and a healthy relationship between hand and mind.
Foreign Languages — Languages are introduced from Class 1 through story, song, and conversation — building an ear for language long before grammar is studied.
________________________________________
Why Eight Years Together Matters
In a world of fragmentation, rushing, and comparison, Waldorf Classes 1–8 offer something radical: continuity, depth, and being truly known. Children are not sorted, ranked, or hurried. They are accompanied — with patience, artistry, and faith in their unfolding — through one of the most important decades of their lives.
Assessment at Maitreya Pathshala is holistic and narrative. Teachers write detailed reports on each child's growth — not reducing a living human being to a number or grade.
At Maitreya Pathshala, in the shadow of the Annapurnas and in the spirit of loving kindness, we hold this vision with deep commitment.

Class 9 and 10
Class 9 — Age 14–15: The Age of Idealism and Critical Awakening
The young person begins to judge the world — and to dream of how it could be.
History and Human Struggles
Class 9 history enters the 19th and 20th centuries — industrialization, colonialism, nationalism, and the World Wars. Students do not merely memorize dates and events. They grapple with the forces behind history: economic power, ideology, human courage, and human failing. Nepali history — the unification of Nepal, the Rana period, the democratic movements — is woven in with depth and pride, giving students a living sense of their own national story.
Literature and Creative Writing
Great literature from both world traditions and Nepali and South Asian voices is read and discussed. Students are encouraged to write — essays, stories, poetry, journals — developing their own authentic voice. Drama, debate, and oral presentation build confidence and articulate expression.
Mathematics — Algebra, Geometry, and Beyond
Mathematics in Class 9 becomes increasingly rigorous and abstract — algebra, coordinate geometry, trigonometry, and statistics are explored not as mechanical procedures but as living tools of thought. Students begin to see mathematics as a language for understanding the universe.
Sciences — Earth, Life, and Matter
Biology deepens into human physiology — students study their own bodies with wonder and scientific precision. Chemistry explores organic chemistry and the materials of the living world. Physics broadens into mechanics, wave theory, and electricity. Wherever possible, learning begins with direct observation and experiment before moving to theory — the Waldorf phenomenological approach continues even as content becomes more advanced.
Geography — Nepal and the World
Nepal's geography — its mountains, river systems, climatic zones, and ecological diversity — is studied with richness and detail. Global geography connects Nepal to world systems of trade, climate, and culture. Students begin to think as global citizens rooted in local understanding.
Arts, Crafts, and Eurythmy
Artistic life continues — painting, sculpture, drama, and music remain integral. Craft work becomes more sophisticated: woodwork, metalwork, and textile arts develop precision and creative pride. Eurythmy continues as a practice of inner and social development.
________________________________________
Class 10 — Age 15–16: Synthesis, Depth, and the SEE
The young person begins to integrate — bringing head, heart, and hands into a coherent self.
Class 10 is a year of integration and culmination. Students bring together everything they have learned — academically, artistically, and humanly — and prepare to step forward into the wider world. It is also the year of the SEE examination, and Maitreya Pathshala prepares students thoroughly and confidently for this milestone.
The Class 10 Project
In the Waldorf tradition, Class 10 students undertake a significant independent project — choosing a subject of deep personal interest, researching it thoroughly, creating something tangible (a written work, a craft piece, a performance, a scientific investigation), and presenting it before the school community and parents. This project is a landmark of self-directed learning and a powerful preparation for higher education and life.
Deepening All Subjects
All core subjects reach their Class 10 depth:
Nepali — Advanced grammar, literature, essay writing, and comprehension. Students engage with classical and contemporary Nepali literature, developing both analytical ability and love for their mother tongue.
English — Reading, writing, grammar, and communication skills are developed to a high standard. Students write essays, letters, reports, and creative pieces with clarity and confidence.
Mathematics — The full Class 10 mathematics curriculum is covered with rigor and understanding: algebra, geometry, trigonometry, mensuration, statistics, and probability. Students learn not just to solve problems but to understand why mathematical truths are true.
Science — Physics, Chemistry, and Biology are each given depth and coherence. Experiments, investigations, and real-world applications bring the sciences alive. Students enter the SEE with both sound theoretical knowledge and genuine scientific curiosity.
Social Studies — Nepal's history, geography, civics, and economics are studied in integrated, meaningful ways — developing informed, thoughtful citizens.
Health, Population, and Environment (HPE) — Students engage seriously with questions of personal health, community wellbeing, ecological responsibility, and Nepal's environmental challenges.

SEE Preparation at Maitreya Pathshala
Strong Foundations, Confident Examination
Maitreya Pathshala takes SEE preparation seriously — not as a replacement for real education, but as a natural expression of it. Students who have been taught to think deeply, write clearly, and understand rather than memorize are, in fact, the best-prepared examination candidates.
In the final months of Class 10, structured SEE preparation is woven into the curriculum:
Regular practice examinations — Full mock SEE papers are sat under examination conditions, giving students experience with timing, format, and exam technique. Results are reviewed carefully with each student to identify areas for focused attention.
Past paper practice — Students work systematically through previous SEE papers in all subjects, becoming thoroughly familiar with question patterns, marking schemes, and effective answering strategies.
Targeted revision blocks — Teachers identify areas where individual students or the whole class need consolidation, and dedicated revision sessions address these with care and precision.
One-to-one support — No student is left behind. Teachers give individual attention to students who need extra support, ensuring every child enters the examination hall with confidence.
Balanced preparation — We believe that an anxious, over-drilled student does not perform their best. Our SEE preparation keeps students calm, motivated, and genuinely engaged — drawing on the inner resilience and love of learning built across nine years of Waldorf education.
Maitreya Pathshala students enter the SEE not with dread, but with readiness — grounded in real understanding, confident in their abilities, and supported by teachers who know them deeply.

The Arts and Wholeness in Classes 9–10
Even as academic demands increase, artistic and practical life never disappears at Maitreya Pathshala. Music, painting, drama, handwork, and eurythmy continue as essential practices — not luxuries. Research consistently shows that students who remain connected to artistic and physical activity through adolescence perform better academically, manage stress more effectively, and develop into more balanced human beings.
The Class 9 and 10 Drama Production is a highlight of school life — a full theatrical performance conceived, rehearsed, designed, and performed by the students themselves. It calls on creativity, courage, collaboration, and commitment in ways that no examination ever could.

After Class 10 — Stepping Forward
Maitreya Pathshala graduates leave Class 10 with:
Their SEE certificate, opening doors to higher secondary education across Nepal and beyond. A strong academic foundation in all core subjects — prepared for the rigors of Classes 11 and 12. A portfolio of artistic and practical work — evidence of creativity, skill, and sustained effort. The inner qualities cultivated across a full Waldorf education: independent thinking, empathy, resilience, creativity, and a genuine love of learning. And perhaps most importantly — a deep knowledge of who they are, and the confidence to become who they are meant to be.

Why These Years Matter So Much
Adolescence is not a problem to be managed — it is a gift to be honored. The young person of 14–16 is undergoing one of the most profound transformations of a human life. At Maitreya Pathshala, we hold this transformation with deep respect, providing both the intellectual rigor and the human warmth that young people truly need.
We do not produce exam machines. We do not produce compliant followers. We accompany young human beings toward their own best selves — and we send them into the world ready.
In the spirit of Maitreya — loving kindness — we walk with them every step of the way.
